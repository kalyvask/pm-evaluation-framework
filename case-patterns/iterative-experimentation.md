# Iterative experimentation under crisis

## The shape

A company faces an existential threat. The default response is to retrench, defend the core, and ride it out. A different response: define explicit success criteria, run small parallel experiments, kill what fails fast, double down on what works. With discipline and pre-committed kill criteria, an existential threat can become a multi-billion-dollar new revenue line.

This is the canonical example of why **iterative experimentation with pre-defined criteria** beats both "play it safe" and "bet the farm" in crisis.

---

## Setup (anonymized)

A gaming platform's flagship business model was eroding. Distribution platforms were taking a larger cut. Player acquisition costs were rising. The company's existing pipeline of titles was losing money on net.

Two paths:

1. **Defend the core.** Cut costs, optimize the existing portfolio, weather the storm.
2. **Run experiments.** Allocate a meaningful but bounded fraction of resources to test new revenue models, new game formats, new distribution channels — with explicit success criteria and kill rules.

The team chose path 2.

## What worked

The discipline was in the **pre-committed criteria**. Before each experiment, the team wrote down:

- **The hypothesis** — what we believe is true
- **The success metric** — specifically what would constitute a win
- **The kill threshold** — specifically what would force us to stop
- **The timeline** — by when we expect to know

Most experiments failed. That was expected. The pre-committed kill criteria meant failed experiments were stopped quickly — within weeks, not quarters — and resources were redeployed.

A small number of experiments showed disproportionate signal. Those were doubled down on. One in particular (a new revenue stream that didn't fit the existing portfolio) grew into a $2–3B/year line of business within two years.

The aggregate result: an existential threat was converted into a meaningful new growth engine. Not by a single bet, but by **systematic, disciplined parallel experimentation**.

## Lessons

### 1. Iteration with discipline beats bet-the-farm

The opposite failure modes are equally bad:

- **Play it safe.** Retrench, defend the core, ride it out. Almost always loses to better-positioned competitors who used the crisis to invest.
- **Bet the farm.** One big strategic pivot. If wrong, existential damage. If right, the company missed an opportunity to learn and the bet was over-sized for the evidence base.

The middle path: **run several small bets in parallel, with explicit success and kill criteria, and concentrate resources on what works.**

### 2. Pre-committed criteria are the discipline that makes iteration work

Without pre-committed criteria, every experiment becomes an opinion fight. *Did this work?* depends on who you ask, what they were rooting for, what narrative they want to be true.

With pre-committed criteria, *did this work?* has a defined answer. The conversation shifts from *"do you feel good about this?"* to *"did we hit the bar we set?"*

This is the discipline that converts experimentation from *thrash* to *learning*.

### 3. Acceptable failure rates

A company running ten experiments in parallel should *expect* to kill 6–8 of them. That is the point. Experiments that all "work" probably weren't testing meaningful hypotheses.

Two implications:

- **Plan for the kill.** When you commit to running an experiment, also commit to the conditions under which you'll kill it. The kill plan is part of the experiment design.
- **Move killed teams quickly to new bets.** A team whose experiment got killed isn't a failed team; they're a team with hard-won learning. Get them onto a new bet within 2–4 weeks (after a postmortem).

### 4. Concentration after signal

Most teams over-spread their bets even after signal emerges. They keep funding the bets that aren't working "in case they turn around" and under-fund the bet that is working "because it might be a fluke."

The right move is the opposite: **concentrate aggressively on what's working** once the signal is clear. The cost of slightly under-funding a winner is huge; the cost of slightly over-funding a fluke is small (you find out and stop).

### 5. Crisis is when conviction matters most

When a company is healthy, conviction is cheap. Everyone can take a bet because the downside is bounded. When a company is in crisis, conviction is expensive — every bet is loaded with stakes, and most of the org wants to cut costs and play defense.

The PMs who run experiments well during crises share a trait: they are willing to **stake credibility** on the experiment design. Not on the *outcome* — outcomes are uncertain — but on the *quality of the criteria*. They commit to the kill rules even when the political cost of killing is high. They commit to the doubling-down even when the political cost of concentrating is high.

This is one of the highest-leverage things a PM can do during a crisis: make the experimentation discipline real, not theatrical.

## When to recognize this pattern in your own work

Useful when:

- Your business model is under existential pressure
- The default response is "play it safe" and you don't have evidence that's the right move
- You have multiple plausible alternative directions but no certainty about which will work
- You can run small bets in parallel without compromising your core operations

Diagnostic questions:

- *What's the bounded resource pool we can put against experiments without compromising the core?*
- *What's our hypothesis for each experiment, and what would success / failure look like?*
- *Are we pre-committing to kill criteria, or will we re-litigate them if the result is mixed?*
- *What's our concentration plan if one of these shows real signal?*
- *Will killed teams move to new bets within 2–4 weeks, or will they linger?*

If two or more of these are unanswered, the experimentation discipline isn't ready. Tighten before launching the experiments.

The pattern's biggest enemy is the *gradient of moral hazard*: incremental commitments without explicit kill criteria. A team that's "still working through the data" three quarters in is not running an experiment. They're running a project that has lost its discipline.
