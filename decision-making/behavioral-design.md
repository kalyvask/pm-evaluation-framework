# Behavioral design

Most products fail not because the strategy is wrong, but because the design works against how humans actually decide.

This file is the bridge between problem framing and the user-facing surface. It names the behavioral principles that determine whether a working strategy translates into a product people actually use. Apply it when reviewing a PRD, design spec, or live feature where the strategy is settled and the design layer is the load-bearing surface.

The frame here is borrowed from behavioral economics and design thinking, but the test is operational: for each principle, can you point at the moment in the user's journey where the design honors it or violates it? If you can't, the principle isn't doing real work in the critique.

---

## The premise

Every product asks the user to make decisions. Sign up or not. Try the feature or skip it. Trust the recommendation or override it. Stay or churn.

Two products with the same strategy and the same feature set can produce very different decisions from the same user, because the *design of the moment* changes what the user actually picks. The PM's job is not just to identify the right problem and the right solution — it's to design the moments so that the user's cognition is working with the product, not against it.

The principles below are the operating layer of that design.

---

## Choice architecture

People decide poorly when overloaded. Five options at a decision point is the rough ceiling; three is often better. Past that, users either default to the most familiar option (which may not be what serves them) or stall.

**The design move:** at any decision moment, collapse the option set to three to five. Hide the long tail behind a "more" affordance for the small number of users who want it. Lead with the option that serves the user's stated goal, not the system's.

**Evaluation question:** at each decision moment in this design, how many options is the user being asked to compare? If more than five, name the cost of the long tail and the case for keeping it.

---

## Defaults

The default option is the single most consequential design choice in any flow. Most users accept it. Pick the wrong default and the design quietly serves the system at the user's expense.

The right default is almost always the choice that serves the user's stated goal, *not* the choice that maximizes a system metric (engagement, retention, revenue). A default that maximizes engagement at the cost of the user's stated goal is a dark pattern in disguise.

**The design move:** explicitly name the default for every decision moment. Justify it from the user's stated goal, not the funnel.

**Evaluation question:** what's the default at each decision moment, and what's the case for it from the user's perspective?

---

## Framing — spend, not violation

Two ways to describe the same deviation from a target:

- *"You went over budget by 280 calories."* (violation)
- *"You spent 280 from your weekly buffer."* (spend)

The first frames the user as having failed. The second frames the user as having spent something they had. Same numbers, different effect on whether the user opens the app tomorrow.

Loss aversion is real — people fear losses more than they value equivalent gains. The PM choice is whether the design uses that asymmetry to motivate forward-looking behavior (spend wisely) or to punish past behavior (you failed). Punitive framing reduces engagement and trains users to hide data from the system.

**The design move:** anywhere a user deviates from a target, frame the deviation as a spend against a finite resource, not a violation of a rule.

**Evaluation question:** is any moment in this design framing user deviation as failure? If yes, can it be reframed as spend without losing the behavioral signal?

---

## Endowment effect

People value things more once they feel ownership of them. The corollary: a goal set by the system has less hold on the user than a goal the user articulated themselves.

**The design move:** in onboarding, let the user name their own goal, in their own words or via their own selection from a small set. Avoid auto-assigning. The system can refine, calibrate, and suggest — but the goal language belongs to the user.

This extends past onboarding. Any user-generated artifact (a saved list, a tagged item, a logged event) carries more weight than the same artifact generated by the system on the user's behalf.

**Evaluation question:** for each user-owned artifact in this design (goals, lists, preferences, history), did the user create it, or did the system create it for them?

---

## Peak-end rule

People do not remember experiences as averages. They remember the peak moment (the most intense, positive or negative) and the end. Both shape what the user takes away, often more than what actually happened in the middle.

**Design implications:**

- The last screen of a flow is more consequential than any single screen in the middle. Design the end deliberately.
- A bad peak (one moment of confusion or anger) can poison an otherwise good experience.
- A celebrated peak (a moment of success, recognition, or surprise) can carry an otherwise mediocre experience.

**The design move:** for any flow, identify the planned peak and the planned end. If they're undesigned (whatever screen happens to be last, whatever moment happens to be most intense), the design is leaving the user's memory to chance.

**Evaluation question:** in this design, what is the peak moment and what is the end? Are both designed, or are they incidental?

---

## Pre-commitment

Willpower is a finite resource, and it depletes during the day. A design that demands the user make a willpower-heavy decision at the moment of weakness (ordering at 8pm, deciding whether to skip the gym at 6am) will lose more often than it wins.

**The design move:** move willpower decisions earlier in the journey, when the user is fresh and goal-oriented. Pre-allocate budgets. Pre-schedule actions. Pre-commit choices when the cost of the choice is lowest.

**Evaluation question:** where in this design is the user being asked to make a willpower-heavy decision? Can it be moved earlier, when the user's resolve is stronger?

---

## Social proof

People look to others to decide what is normal or trustworthy. The design pattern — *"X people like you did Y"* — works, but it has two failure modes:

1. **Wrong reference group.** If "people like you" is computed too broadly, the social proof feels generic. If too narrowly, the population is too small and the signal becomes noise.
2. **Backfire on choices the user feels conflicted about.** Showing "60% of people ordered the burger" to a user trying to eat lighter reinforces the wrong norm.

**The design move:** social proof should be feature-flagged and tested before defaulting to on. The right reference group is goal-aligned, not demographically broad. When in doubt, leave it off.

**Evaluation question:** is social proof used in this design? If yes, what's the reference group, and is it goal-aligned with the user being shown the proof?

---

## Flow

A user in flow stays engaged when the cognitive load of the moment matches their capability. Glanceable surfaces (three seconds of attention) feel effortless. Deep surfaces (thirty seconds of attention) feel meaningful when the user has chosen to be there, and feel tiring when imposed.

**The design move:** the default surface for any feature should be glanceable. Depth should be one tap away, never the front door. The exception is a deliberate deep-work surface, where the user has signed up for sustained attention.

**Evaluation question:** what's the cognitive load of this surface at the default level? Can a user understand the state of their world in three seconds, or do they have to read?

---

## Friction as a design tool

Friction is not a sin. It's a design instrument. Used in the right place, it protects accuracy (editing a permission), filters intent (committing to a goal), or signals importance (paying for a service). Used in the wrong place, it kills adoption.

**The design move:** for each moment that has friction in the design, name whose interest the friction serves. Friction that serves the user (commitment, accuracy, importance) is justified. Friction that serves the system (data collection, gated upsell, forced consent) is suspect.

The rule of thumb: friction at the moment of commitment is usually right. Friction at the moment of first value is usually wrong.

**Evaluation question:** for each friction point in this design, whose interest does it serve? If the answer is "the system's," can the friction be moved or removed without breaking the system goal?

## Frequency dictates UI density

A daily task should compress to one screen with low friction. An email send, a quick log, a routine check. The user has the muscle memory; the design should serve it, not interrupt it.

An annual task should expand into a shepherded sequence of steps with deliberate friction. Filing taxes, setting up an estate plan, the once-a-year HR review. The user is afraid, the stakes are high, and the design buys them confidence by going slowly and explaining each step. TurboTax is the canonical reference: a long sequence of small, friendly questions instead of one intimidating form.

The mistake is applying daily-task patterns to annual-task moments (one form with thirty fields for tax filing) or applying annual-task patterns to daily-task moments (a five-step wizard to send an email). Both produce friction in the wrong direction.

**The design move:** estimate the frequency of each task in this product. Compress the daily ones. Shepherd the annual ones. Don't apply the same pattern to both.

**Evaluation question:** how often does the user do this task? If daily, is the surface compressed enough for muscle memory? If annual, is it shepherded enough for the user to feel taken care of?

---

## Sacrifice edge cases for the 80 percent

Good engineers find edge cases. Good designers decide which ones to make harder so the 80 percent flow stays simple.

The Apple vs. early-Android example is the canonical one: early Android exposed every configurable option a user might want; Apple constrained the surface. The constrained surface was usable for most people most of the time. The configurable surface was usable for power users and tiring for everyone else. Power users like the configurable one. Everyone else doesn't last.

The discipline is honest about the trade. Some users genuinely need the edge case. The design move is not to remove it entirely — it's to move it off the primary surface so the default flow stays clean. Settings menus, advanced affordances, progressive disclosure, "show more" expansions, and power-user modes are the tools.

**The design move:** for each edge case currently on the primary surface, ask whether it's serving the 80 percent or the 20 percent. If the latter, move it to a secondary surface without losing the users who depend on it.

**Evaluation question:** what edge cases is this design serving on the primary surface? Could they be moved to a secondary surface without breaking the workflow of the users who need them?

## Mental models

Users arrive at a product with pre-existing beliefs about how the underlying system works. Those beliefs are often wrong — but they shape every interaction. *The AC has a temperature dial.* (Most don't; they have on/off and a thermostat target.) *Antivirus software scans the computer while it's shut down.* (No.) *Closing the app stops the background process.* (Sometimes, sometimes not.)

These mental models persist even when the product behaves otherwise. A user who believes the system works one way will interpret unexpected behavior as a *bug* before they revise the model. The product can be objectively correct and still feel broken because the mental model says it should do something different.

Three responses, only two of which work:

- **Work with the existing mental model.** Match the user's expectations even if the underlying mechanism is different. The product feels intuitive because the surface matches what the user already believes. Most consumer products that feel "magical" are doing this.
- **Teach a new mental model — deliberately.** When the mechanism is genuinely novel and the user can't continue without understanding it, surface the model. An inline diagram, an onboarding flow, a "how this works" panel. Rubrik exposes parts of its black box this way so admins can build accurate expectations. Required when AI changes how an existing surface behaves (the user's model needs an update before the AI's outputs make sense).
- **Ignore the mental model.** Build the product so the user has to figure out the mechanism on their own. This is the default failure mode in engineering-led products. The team's blameless name for it is "user education." The user's name for it is "this is confusing."

A useful diagnostic: ask three target users to describe, in their own words, what they think the product does when they take a specific action. If the descriptions diverge from the actual mechanism in ways that matter, the design has to either move to match them or teach them. Choosing not to choose is the failure mode.

**Evaluation question:** what mental model does this product require the user to hold? Is the design working with their existing one, deliberately teaching a new one, or assuming they'll figure it out?

## System 1 and System 2

Two cognitive modes operate in tandem:

- **System 1** — fast, intuitive, automatic. Drives most everyday choices. Uses heuristics and emotional responses.
- **System 2** — slow, analytical, deliberate. Engages when stakes, complexity, or novelty force it.

Most product moments are decided in System 1. Design that demands System 2 attention for routine choices (a complicated form for a casual action) feels tiring. Design that lets System 1 handle routine choices and surfaces System 2 attention only when the stakes warrant it (a confirmation step before deleting a payment method) feels right.

**Evaluation question:** for each decision moment, which system is the design asking the user to engage? Is the match appropriate to the stakes?

---

## Nudges and dark patterns

The line between a nudge and a dark pattern is whether the design works for or against the user's stated goal.

A nudge guides the user toward an action that serves their stated goal, while preserving their freedom to choose otherwise. *Setting the most beneficial option as the default is a nudge.* The user can opt out with one action, and the system would still tell them they did the right thing if they did.

A dark pattern engineers the user into an action that serves the system at the user's expense. *Confirm-shame, fake urgency, hidden costs, forced consent flows.* These exploit System 1 to drive an action the user would not choose under System 2 reflection.

The legal risk is real (FTC, EU consumer law). The brand risk is larger. The product risk is largest — dark patterns damage trust permanently, and trust is the single hardest input to rebuild.

**Evaluation question:** for each persuasive design choice in this product, would the user feel respected by it or manipulated by it if they noticed it consciously? If the answer is "manipulated," it's a dark pattern, regardless of intent.

---

## The crossroad test

A meta-diagnostic that runs across all the principles above: at every decision point in a flow, can the user answer *"what's next"* without thinking?

If the user has to pause, re-read, or check elsewhere to figure out what to do next, the design has failed at that moment. The cost is not just the friction of the pause — the user has dropped out of System 1 and into System 2 for a decision that should have been automatic. Repeated System 2 demands fatigue the user and damage the experience cumulatively, even if no single moment is fatal.

The test is binary. Walk through each crossroad in the design. Ask *"what should the user do next?"* If the answer requires a sentence of explanation rather than being obvious from the screen, the moment needs redesign.

This is also the cleanest test for whether a design is genuinely simple or just sparse. Sparse designs can still fail the crossroad test when the user reaches a moment and doesn't know which of three plausible actions to take. Genuinely simple designs make the right action obvious without the user having to compare options.

**Evaluation question:** at each crossroad in this design, is *"what's next"* obvious from the screen alone, or does it require the user to think? If thinking is required, what specifically is missing — the affordance, the label, the visual hierarchy, the prior context?

---

## Principles in tension

Some principles pull against each other, and the PM has to make a posture decision rather than try to honor all of them everywhere.

Common tensions:

- **Choice architecture vs. autonomy.** Collapsing options serves clarity; preserving long-tail access serves autonomy. The right call depends on whether the user is in a casual moment or a power-user moment.
- **Pre-commitment vs. real-time agency.** Pre-allocating decisions reduces willpower load but takes choice away from the live moment. Power users often resent it.
- **Defaults vs. ownership.** Good defaults serve most users; user-set choices serve engagement. The right call depends on how confidence-bearing the default is.
- **Friction vs. flow.** Adding friction protects accuracy; removing it preserves momentum. Hard to do both at the same moment.

When the design is in tension, the right output is a stated *posture* — "this design optimizes for low friction at the cost of deep commitment" — not a claim that the design serves every principle.

---

## Checklist

Run this before any design review:

1. **Choice architecture:** how many options at each decision moment? More than five is a flag.
2. **Defaults:** what's the default at each moment, and does it serve the user's stated goal?
3. **Framing:** are deviations framed as spend or as violation?
4. **Endowment:** which artifacts in this product are user-created vs. system-created?
5. **Peak and end:** is the peak moment designed? Is the end designed?
6. **Pre-commitment:** are willpower-heavy decisions placed at the moment of weakness, or moved earlier?
7. **Social proof:** if used, is the reference group goal-aligned?
8. **Flow:** is the default surface glanceable in three seconds?
9. **Friction:** does each friction point serve the user's interest, or the system's?
10. **Frequency match:** does UI density match task frequency (compressed for daily, shepherded for annual)?
11. **Edge cases:** are edge cases on the primary surface, or moved off it so the 80 percent flow stays clean?
12. **Mental models:** does the design work with the user's existing mental model, teach a new one deliberately, or assume they'll figure it out?
13. **Crossroad test:** at every decision point, can the user answer "what's next" without thinking?
14. **Posture:** when principles are in tension, has the design taken a stated posture, or is it pretending to honor all of them?

If two or more of these are vague, the design needs another pass before the review.
