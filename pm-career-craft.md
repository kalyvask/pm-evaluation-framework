# PM career craft

The PM craft includes managing your own career. The same intellectual honesty, structured thinking, and bias for action that produces good products produces a good career. This file covers the working-PM patterns for networking, getting into the room, performing well in interviews, and moving deliberately between roles.

The bias here is toward what compounds: relationships that survive job changes, reputation that travels, and decisions that improve the *next* decision rather than just this one.

---

## Networking: the graduated-ask pattern

Networking is a long compounding game. Treat it like one.

The default failure mode is the cold ask for too much. *"Can we set up a 30-minute coffee chat?"* sent to someone who doesn't know you yet. Most senior PMs receive several of these per week from strangers. The default reaction is to ignore, because the cost of responding is high and the signal is low.

The pattern that works: graduated asks, starting with something the recipient can respond to in two minutes.

1. **First touch.** A two-minute, specific question on something they said in public (a podcast, a post, a panel). Not *"can we connect"*. A real question that demonstrates you read the source. Reply cost is low; if they reply, you have a thread.
2. **Follow through.** When their advice helps (or doesn't), report back briefly. This demonstrates that you act on input rather than collect it. It also gives them a positive signal that costs them nothing.
3. **Iterate.** Each subsequent interaction earns slightly more room. Eventually some of these become real working relationships. A few become friends.
4. **Reciprocate.** Offer value back when you can. Junior people can help senior people more than they think: introductions, things they wouldn't have seen, sharp questions that clarify their thinking.

The principle: relationships compound on small, repeated, value-positive interactions. Asking for half an hour without first establishing that pattern is asking the relationship to start at the top of the trust curve.

Mentorship specifically: **don't ask.** The *"will you be my mentor?"* framing is the relationship version of proposing on a first date. Mentorship emerges from repeated useful interaction. The right move is to be the kind of person someone would want to mentor, then let the relationship form on its own.

## The back-channel effect

Reputation is portable. The same industry that's hard to break into is small enough that everyone has back channels.

Two implications:

1. **Don't burn bridges.** Ever. The colleague you snub today is the back channel five years from now when someone is deciding whether to hire you. The most senior PMs have learned this; some learned it the hard way. The cheapest unforced error in a PM career is being dismissive of someone you don't think matters today.
2. **Manage the back-channel layer deliberately.** When you do good work, make sure the people who would back-channel you can articulate what you did and why it mattered. Most managers and peers cannot describe your contribution in a sentence; if they cannot, they cannot recommend you. Tell them the version of your work that is both accurate and concise enough to repeat to someone else.

The longer-arc principle: the people most likely to vouch for you ten years from now are people you worked alongside as peers when you were both junior. The pool of references you build at any given career stage is mostly a function of how you treated *peers* at earlier career stages, not how you treated leaders at the current stage.

## Breaking in: hustle, wedges, and side doors

Most first PM jobs aren't won through the front door.

The pattern:

1. **Use a strength as the wedge.** Domain expertise, technical depth, customer-facing experience, knowledge of a specific market. The wedge gets you into conversations the resume alone wouldn't. It positions the conversation as *"I already know X that you need, and I'm trying to do PM work; let's find a way to overlap"* rather than *"please consider me for a job I haven't earned yet."*
2. **Demonstrate value before the title.** Build a prototype the company would benefit from. Write a teardown of their product with sharp, specific suggestions. Ship something for them on contract before they hire you full-time. The cost of doing the work is small relative to the signal it sends.
3. **Side door beats front door for first roles.** A PM job at a Series A startup where someone takes a chance on you is usually accessible without the canonical PM-experience requirement. Once you are in, the path to bigger roles widens.
4. **Hustle on quantity.** Most attempts won't work. You need one to work. The PMs who break in are the ones who tried many more times than the ones who didn't.

The principle: an interview is a signal that someone has decided to evaluate you. Most career moves are won before the interview, by reducing the perceived risk of betting on you. Demonstrate the work; the title follows.

## Interview signal: what high-confidence hires look like

Three signals that distinguish strong PM candidates from average ones.

### 1. Communication under structured ambiguity

The single most common kill in PM interviews: brilliant candidate, can't communicate. Specifically can't structure a long answer such that the listener can take notes. Symptoms: lots of detail without altitude management, acronyms without context, an unclear hand-off if the prompt is *"explain this to me as if I were an intern who'd take over the project."*

The fix is practice, not talent. Explain a recent project end-to-end to a friend who doesn't know the domain. If they cannot take notes, the explanation isn't structured. Iterate.

A useful structural tool: number your points before you make them. *"There are three things I want to cover. First..."* This sounds rote; it works. The listener can take notes. The first point lands; the second lands on top of the first. Without numbering, points dissolve into prose.

### 2. High agency calibrated to stakes

The interviewer is looking for: can this candidate scope an exploration appropriately, or do they default to the same answer regardless of the question?

The weak version is *"I'll talk to 30 users and synthesize."* Always 30 users. Always synthesis. The strong version is *"given this is a reversible software two-way door with a fast iteration cycle, I'd talk to three users and ship the next hypothesis within a week. If it were a multi-year platform decision, I'd want segmentation data and ~30 users across the segments."*

The principle: agency is the willingness to right-size the work to the stakes, and to justify the calibration explicitly. See [`decision-making/research-methods.md`](decision-making/research-methods.md) § "Right-sizing exploration to stakes" for the underlying framing.

### 3. Authentic enthusiasm

Generic interest loses. Specific, demonstrated interest wins.

A candidate who has used the product, has thoughts about it, has read the company's strategy, has watched a relevant talk, and can name *one specific reason this company at this moment* wins against a more credentialed candidate whose answers are generic. Especially at companies where the founders or hiring managers are visible in public, the candidate who shows they engaged with the public material has done research the more credentialed candidate hasn't.

The discipline: before any interview, do enough research that you can name one specific person, decision, or moment that draws you to this company. If you cannot, you haven't done the work.

## Company over title (early-career)

A common early-career failure: optimizing for the title.

The better optimization, especially in the first 5-10 years: optimize for the *next* interview, not this one. The right company is the one that, two years from now, gives you the strongest story for the job after this. That usually points to growth-stage companies with strong leadership and visible momentum. Titles adjust internally once you are in; the company you joined is permanent in your story.

Two specific patterns:

- **Learning compounds in growth.** You learn more in two years at a company that is growing 4x than in four years at one that is stable. Pick the trajectory, not the title.
- **Stable role plus falling company is the worst combination.** When the company isn't growing, internal mobility tightens, mentors leave, and the story you can tell about your work weakens. The two-year mark in a non-growing company is usually the right time to leave.

In the era of unstable role labels (deployed PM, applied PM, founding PM, AI PM, forward-deployed engineer), the *company* signal travels better than the *role* signal. Pick accordingly.

## Internal moves: earn the right, then ask

The pattern that works inside a company:

1. Prove you can do the job you have.
2. Take on additional, visible work outside the role's scope. Volunteer for hard problems.
3. Build allies who'd vouch for you in a review cycle or a reference call.
4. Then make the ask.

The pattern that fails: arriving at a new company and asking for a different role within the first month. The hiring manager has just spent capital on you for the role you're in; you haven't proven you can execute on it; and the ask burns the trust that took the prior conversation to build.

A useful adjacent pattern: **build a brand by doing the hard work others avoid.** Every company has work that nobody wants but somebody needs. Volunteering for it, even if you don't fully know how to solve it, earns disproportionate reputation. Becoming the person leadership turns to when something is on fire is the most durable career asset in a long PM tenure. It also accumulates back-channel currency faster than any other move.

## When this file applies

Use this file when:

- You are searching for your next PM role
- You are about to enter an interview process and want to calibrate signal
- You are deciding between offers
- You are planning an internal move
- You are building your network and want a deliberate pattern, not just attendance at events

Don't use this file when:

- The career question is about a specific decision unique to your situation. The answer to specific career questions is usually a conversation with a mentor or trusted colleague, not a generic framework. Use this file to prepare for that conversation, not to replace it.
- You are looking for a recruiting workflow (resume formatting, application volume, ATS optimization). That layer is real but separate; the principles above sit one level above the workflow.
