# Strategy memo rubric

A rubric for evaluating a PM strategy memo — the kind you'd send up the chain when proposing a product change, a new feature, an acquisition integration, or a pivot.

The shape of the artifact:

- A description of the current product and its context
- The problem or opportunity, with evidence
- A recommendation
- What's required to implement, at the level of strategic roadmap (not project plan)
- Key tradeoffs, considered honestly

This rubric works for internal exec memos, board updates, strategy proposals, and the written brief that backs an exec review.

---

## What a strong memo includes, in order of importance

1. **Current state** — what the product does, who uses it, how the company makes money on it, the competitive landscape. Not exhaustive — *load-bearing context only*.
2. **The problem or opportunity** — concrete, specific, evidenced.
3. **The recommendation** — your call. Owned. Defensible.
4. **What it takes to ship** — selected roadmap elements, not a full plan.
5. **Tradeoffs and considerations** — customers, stakeholders, legal, brand, strategy, competition, financials. Most important first.

The "in order of importance" matters. A strong problem framing with a thin solution beats a weak problem framing with an extensive solution.

---

## Evaluation criteria, in order of importance

### 1. Depth and quality of analysis

**The most important criterion.**

**Strong:**
- Goes beyond surface observation. Identifies a non-obvious tension, a structural constraint, or a counterintuitive insight that changes how the reader thinks about the problem.
- The reasoning chain is visible and falsifiable. Each step is something a thoughtful reader could agree or disagree with on the merits.
- Demonstrates real engagement with the product and the market — not a Wikipedia summary dressed up.

**Adequate:**
- Correct but unsurprising. Restates well-known facts about the product / market.
- Reasoning is implicit; the reader has to reconstruct the chain.

**Weak:**
- Generic — could be applied to any product in the category.
- Reasoning is asserted, not shown.
- Heavy on description, light on insight.

### 2. Identification and depth of key tradeoffs

**Strong:**
- Identifies the *most important* tradeoffs, not just *all* of them.
- For each tradeoff: what's gained, what's lost, in concrete terms.
- One or two tradeoffs are explored in depth. What's lost is real, not hand-waved.
- Risks and uncertainties are flagged where they belong, not buried.

**Adequate:**
- Multiple tradeoffs listed; one or two explored at moderate depth.
- The most important tradeoff might be present but not foregrounded.

**Weak:**
- Tradeoffs listed at uniform depth; none explored.
- The "this might fail" section is performative — no real risk surfaced.
- No prioritization signal — every consideration weighted equally.

### 3. Clarity of problem, solution, and implementation

**Strong:**
- Problem stated in concrete, falsifiable terms (specific user, specific job, specific blocker, specific cost).
- Solution described in user-outcome terms, not feature lists. The reader can imagine the user's experience.
- Implementation section names the load-bearing elements without descending into project-management detail.

**Adequate:**
- Problem and solution clear but somewhat generic.
- Implementation section comprehensive but not differentiated by importance.

**Weak:**
- Problem described as a feature request ("the product needs an X").
- Solution is a feature list with no user-outcome through-line.
- Implementation section either missing or unhelpfully detailed (Gantt charts, full timelines).

## What an exec audience weighs, that PMs often miss

### Risk sections that surface real risk, not generic risk

A common failure: a "Risks" section that lists generic items ("execution risk," "competitive risk," "regulatory risk"). That's not risk analysis. That's a risk *taxonomy*.

A useful risk section says: *"The plan depends on top-tier customers adopting; we don't have evidence they will. If they don't, here's what we'd do."* It names the *load-bearing assumption*, the *signal that would falsify it*, and the *contingency*.

### Pre-committed success criteria

Strong memos name specific metrics, specific values, specific timelines for what counts as success. Weak memos wave at "growth in adoption" or "improved engagement" without committing.

The pre-commitment is what turns a recommendation into a real bet, not a presentation.

### A clear point of view, owned

Exec audiences reward *taking a position*. The memo should be defensible by a single author who would say, in Q&A: *"Yes, I considered the alternatives. I picked this one because [reason]. I'd revise if I saw [signal]."*

Memos that present three options weighted equally usually score worse than memos that pick one and defend it. The author's job is to demonstrate judgment.

### Specificity over generality

A strong recommendation says: *"In the next quarter, ship X to segment Y, measured by metric Z, with a kill threshold of W."* A weak recommendation says: *"Improve the product for users."*

Specificity is harder. It commits the author to falsifiable claims. That's the point.

### What's *not* in scope

A strong memo explicitly names what it's *not* trying to address. *"We are deliberately not addressing X, Y, Z in this proposal. Here's why."* This signals prioritization.

A weak memo silently tries to address everything. *If everything is important, nothing is.*

## Common failure patterns

- **Wikipedia summary mode.** First half of the memo is "what the product does" with little analytical content.
- **Feature laundry list.** Solution is a list of 8 things to build, no through-line.
- **Risk taxonomy without risk analysis.** "Risks: execution, competitive, regulatory."
- **Generic success metrics.** "We'll measure user satisfaction and engagement."
- **No tradeoffs.** Every alternative presented as obviously inferior.
- **Insider-knowledge-as-analysis.** "Internally, we know X" — without separating insider knowledge from open analysis.
- **Implementation Gantt-charting.** Full project plan instead of strategic roadmap elements.
- **Heroic feature.** A single magical feature that solves all the problems.

When two or more of these show up, the memo reads as a presentation rather than a recommendation.

## Practical scoring guidance

For a 25-point scale (matching the PM evaluation rubric):

| Range | Profile |
|---|---|
| 22–25 | Strong analysis, clear position, defensible tradeoffs, falsifiable success criteria. Ready for exec review. |
| 17–21 | Solid recommendation with one or two gaps. Either the analysis depth or the tradeoff treatment needs another pass. |
| 12–16 | Functional but missing material elements. Often: weak risk analysis, generic success criteria, no kill criterion. |
| 5–11 | Closer to a description than a recommendation. The author has not yet made the bet. |

When the score is below 17, the most leverage usually comes from re-working the *problem identification* and *tradeoff analysis* sections. The memo below that bar is rarely fixable by tightening the solution paragraph.

---

## How to use this rubric

For self-review: score your own memo. The lowest-scoring criterion is what to re-work first.

For peer feedback: walk through the criteria with a colleague. The conversation about *why* a score is what it is, is more valuable than the score itself.

For exec readiness checks: before sending up the chain, score against this rubric. A memo below 17 usually doesn't survive the exec Q&A; better to take another pass.
