# Product review rubric

A rubric for evaluating how a PM shows up in a product review, exec strategy meeting, or live decision discussion — the kind where you cold-defend a position, build on others' contributions, and revise in real time.

This isn't about performing. Performative participation is usually the worst kind. The rubric describes what *useful* contribution looks like.

---

## Five dimensions of strong participation

### 1. Listening and building

> Does the participant track the thread of the conversation and build on it?

**Strong:** Visibly tracks what's been said. References specific points others made. Builds *on* (not *over*) prior contributions.

**Adequate:** Adds new points without explicitly engaging the prior thread. Doesn't contradict, doesn't extend.

**Weak:** Ignores the conversation flow. Re-states points already made. Asks questions that were just answered.

### 2. Analytical grounding

> Are the comments grounded in the actual situation, or are they general opinion?

**Strong:** References specific facts (data, user feedback, the operating context). Distinguishes evidence from inference. Names the source when uncertain ("the data shows" vs. "I think").

**Adequate:** Mostly grounded but occasionally drifts into general opinion or anecdote.

**Weak:** Mostly opinion or anecdote. Vague references to "what users want." Ignores the specifics in front of the team.

### 3. Engagement beyond restatement

> Does the contribution move the conversation forward?

**Strong:** Introduces a framework, identifies a tension the discussion is glossing over, draws a connection to a different pattern, or surfaces a stakeholder perspective the room missed.

**Adequate:** Adds new analytical content but mostly within the existing frame.

**Weak:** Re-states facts without analysis. Adds detail without insight. The contribution doesn't move anything forward.

### 4. Position-taking and revision

> Does the participant take a clear position, defend it, and revise it when challenged with evidence?

**Strong:** States a clear position. Defends with reasoning, not assertion. Revises when challenged with evidence — visibly and graciously.

**Adequate:** States a position but defends with re-assertion rather than reasoning. Revises in response to seniority or volume rather than evidence.

**Weak:** Hedges every position. Or — equally bad — refuses to revise even when challenged with strong evidence.

### 5. Advancing collective understanding

> Does the contribution help the room reach a better decision?

**Strong:** Surfaces ambiguity the room was glossing over. Asks well-posed questions that re-frame the conversation. Bridges between perspectives that seemed incompatible.

**Adequate:** Adds individual analytical contributions, but the room-level dynamic doesn't shift.

**Weak:** Contributions don't connect to others. The discussion doesn't move forward as a result.

## Behaviors that strong participants do

### They take positions

The hardest thing in a product review is *committing* to a position when you're not sure. Hedging is the default — *"on the one hand, on the other hand, more research needed."* It feels safe and analytical. It is neither.

A strong participant says: *"I'd go with option 2. Here's why. Here's what I'd want to validate. Here's what I'd revise on if I saw X."* Specific, defensible, falsifiable.

### They challenge respectfully and with reasoning

Disagreement is not an attack. *"I think the data shows us something different — let me test that against your point"* is more useful than *"that's true, and also..."*

Any challenge should include the *reasoning*, not just the *position*. *"I disagree because"* — fill in the rest.

### They ask good questions

Well-posed questions are as valuable as assertions. The right question can re-direct an entire decision. Examples:

- *"What does the data tell us about the strategic segment, vs. the segment we actually validated with?"*
- *"Are we treating this as a friction problem when the evidence suggests it's an incentive problem?"*
- *"Which of these is a one-way door? I think we're treating them all the same."*

A good question is specific, anchored in the situation, and creates a fork in the conversation.

### They draw on outside experience appropriately

When someone has worked on a similar problem elsewhere, that experience is enriching when it:

- Connects the current decision to a similar pattern
- Surfaces a stakeholder perspective the room is missing
- Adds a constraint that's real in the field but not yet on the table

It's *not* enriching when used to:

- "At my old company, we did X" — without connecting to the present decision
- Anecdote-driven assertion without engaging the current evidence
- Hijacking the discussion with personal experience that doesn't translate

The bar: outside experience should *enrich the decision*, not *replace the analysis*.

### They manage airtime

Quality matters more than frequency. Airtime should be *roughly proportional to the value being added*. Two great contributions in a 75-minute review is often better than ten mediocre ones.

Specifically:

- Don't speak just to be heard
- Don't dominate when others have something to add
- Don't disappear when you have something specific to contribute

### They distinguish surfacing ambiguity from solving

Surfacing ambiguity is often as valuable as proposing a solution. In a real exec review, naming the elephant in the room (*"we don't actually know how the strategic segment will react to this"*) is a higher-leverage contribution than proposing a solution that elides the ambiguity.

A strong participant is comfortable saying: *"I'm not sure what the right answer is. Here's the ambiguity I think matters most. Here's how I'd resolve it before committing."*

## Behaviors that weak participants do

A non-exhaustive list:

- **Restating context as if it were analysis**
- **Using "I think" to soften assertions** that are actually well-grounded ("I think users care about UX" — yes, of course)
- **Hedging every position** to avoid being wrong
- **Taking positions without reasoning**, treating volume or seniority as substitute for argument
- **Refusing to revise** when presented with new evidence
- **Hijacking with personal anecdotes** that don't translate
- **Asking questions whose answer is already on the table**
- **Speaking just to be heard** — adding nothing the prior speaker didn't already say
- **Failing to listen** — making points that contradict or ignore the conversation flow

---

## What senior participants weigh, when evaluating others

When a senior PM, engineering leader, or exec evaluates someone in a product review, they're weighing:

1. **Did they actually take a position, or did they hedge?**
2. **When challenged, did they defend with reasoning or with reassertion?**
3. **Did they listen and build, or did they monologue?**
4. **Did they ground their points in the actual evidence, or did they drift into general opinion?**
5. **Did they advance the room's understanding, or just the air-time count?**

Note that none of these criteria are about *being right*. Strong participation in a product review is not about winning the argument. It's about *thinking well in public*, with humility about uncertainty and conviction about the things you've reasoned through.

---

## How to use this rubric

For self-review: after a review, score your own contributions. The lowest score is the area to work on next time.

For mentoring more junior PMs: walk through the dimensions after a session. Specific feedback ("you took a position but defended it with re-assertion, not reasoning — what was the actual reason you preferred that option?") beats general feedback.

For interview / panel evaluation: this rubric is also a reasonable proxy for what hiring panels evaluate when running PM case interviews. The dimensions are the same; the stakes are different.
